06 Assumptions & Notes

Method & assumptions

  1. Method follows Niu, Airframe Stress Analysis & Sizing, Sections 6.4 & 6.5 — load redistribution around a skin cut-out: fixed-end-moment frame idealisation for cabin-pressure tension, plus flat-plate liquidating-shear for in-plane (flight) shear. [SCT-025]
  2. Door capability factors ηh / ηl default to 0 = full Niu redistribution (the plug door transfers all hoop / longitudinal load to the surrounding frames & sills). Set a non-zero value only with a substantiated door load path — ηh > 0 reduces the redistributed frame load and is unconservative without justification. [deviates from Niu §6.5]
  3. General bays (Niu §6.5): upper a, lower b, forward c, aft d are independent. Each corner panel (a×c, a×d, b×c, b×d) and each side has its own shear / axial — corner shears, edge redistribution and member loads are evaluated per location and only the governing (worst) value feeds the headline. The original spreadsheet assumed equal corners; the full per-corner / per-side breakdown is tabulated in Appendix A (reference only). Frame & sill member axial loads already reflect the different auxiliary distances (frame → c/d, sill → a/b). The default geometry reproduces Niu's worked example: the Case I body-shear chain gives q₁/q₂/q₃ = 1108/932/255 lbf/in (bands = 450, independent of P); the Case III pressure chain reproduces Niu at P = 17.2 psi (corner moment 169,198 in·lb, upper-fwd corner 210.2 lbf/in). Headline values are the governing (worst) location.
  4. Section properties — neutral-axis offset ȳ and second moment of area I must be derived separately from the built-up frame + bear-strap + skin section. Enter the computed values.
  5. Worst-case frame hoop load takes the greater of the stop-fitting load and the cut-out redistribution load. If the stop-fitting load is the lesser, stop loads are insufficient to react frame tension and the frame pulls inward against the door until stop-fitting loads rise to match frame tension (free-edge equilibrium).
  6. Inner-chord stress is lower than nominal hoop due to bending compression, but this is localised at the frame ends; the nominal hoop stress is retained as the conservative peak value.
  7. Body shear (Case I) — the fuselage skin shear flow is diverted around the cut-out per Niu §6.5. Enter four band shear flows: qa (upper aux), qb (upper main), qc (lower main), qd (lower aux). Equal bands → uniform shear (cut-out on the neutral axis, Fig 6.5.3/6.5.4) — validated against Niu's worked example (450 → q₁/q₂/q₃ = 1108/932/255 lbf/in). Unequal bands → variable shear for off-NA cut-outs (floor / cargo, Fig 6.5.6–6.5.8): the cut-out height splits hb=a·h/(a+b), hc=b·h/(a+b) and the upper/lower panels redistribute their own band shear. Headline checks use the governing (worst) panel. [Niu §6.5 Case I]
  8. Band shears from vertical load (optional) — entering a non-zero fuselage vertical shear V auto-computes the four bands from a thin-walled circular-fuselage shear distribution q=V·cosθ/(k·2πR), taking the effective shear area of the skin as the exact thin-ring value k·A = 0.5·A (A = full skin area), giving qmax=V/πR at the N.A. q is max at the N.A. and →0 at the crown. The geometry is circumferential — the cut-out height h, bays and the lower-main-sill offset y0 are arc distances along the skin from the N.A. — so each band's arc position is mapped to angle θ=arc/R (equivalent vertical height y=R·sinθ) before evaluating q at the band centroid. The four band inputs then become read-only. Bare-skin idealisation — not necessarily conservative: a uniform-shell first estimate that ignores the cabin floor beam and stringers; in a real fuselage these divert bending and can raise the side-skin shear above the bare-ring value, so replace qa..qd with an internal-loads / FEM shear-flow distribution (manual entry, V=0) when available. The shear is assumed to act through the section centroid (an offset adds a torsional shear, not included); centroid sampling is within ~3% of the band average. A band reaching the crown (arc ≥ ¼-circle, θ ≥ 90°) is flagged and its q forced to 0. Set V = 0 to enter the bands manually.
  9. Corner peaking — the panel shears q₁/q₂/q₃ and their gross-area stresses are bay-average; the shear migrates to the stiff main sills, so the actual peak at the cut-out corners is ~+50% (two stringer bays, 3:1) up to ~+67% (three bays, 5:3:1). Apply ~1.5–1.67× to the average for local peak skin stress / corner-detail fatigue.
  10. In-plane shear member axial (Niu §6.4, flat-plate / large R) — shear redistributing around the cut-out develops cap (liquidating) axial in the bounding members, axial = q × ½(cut-out dimension): ΔPframe = qfr·h/2 with qfr = ½(qb+qc); ΔPsill = qb·ℓ/2 (upper) or qc·ℓ/2 (lower), governing = max. It is bay-independent (only ℓ, h, the bands and member areas matter), peaks at the cut-out corners, and reduces to q₀·½(dim) when bands are equal. The load is ± (tension on one diagonal corner pair); the tension value is taken and added to the pressure member axial, so the Member Stresses cards read the combined operating member tension. Shear axial is pure cap tension — it adds no extra bending. [Niu §6.4]
  11. Damage-tolerance use: enter P as the operating cabin-pressure differential and the body-shear bands at the typical (1g) flight level — results are operating stresses for crack-growth / residual-strength work, not ultimate design loads.

Appendix notes

  1. These per-location values are computed for completeness but are not used directly. The headline Key Results take the governing (worst) of each set: the smallest corner panel for corner skin shear (highest q), and the largest member axial across fwd/aft frame and upper/lower sill.
  2. The adjusted corner moment Madj [10] is bay-independent (Niu's distribution factors use only h, ℓ); the corner shear differs purely through the corner area. The frame & sill axial loads vary with the auxiliary distances (frame → c/d; sill → a/b) — so unequal aux bays are fully reflected in the member stresses.
